Part 2: Downlink Network Topology

The Two-Segment Split Topology

Cooperative Timing & Frequency Control

3GPP specifications decouple the end-to-end communication path into two isolated segments. This isolates ground-based and space-based physics completely:

🚀 Segment 1: The Feeder Link (Gateway-to-Sat) Links the ground gateway antenna with the satellite payload over S/Ka-band. Because the gateway coordinates are fixed and the satellite's trajectory is highly deterministic, the **Ground Gateway/gNB handles 100% of this segment's Timing & Doppler compensation**.
📡 Segment 2: The Service Link (Sat-to-UE Handset) Links the satellite directly to the consumer mobile phone (UE). Since UEs are moving dynamically and are dispersed throughout the beam spot, **the UE must autonomously compute and pre-compensate** for Timing Advance (Common TA) and Doppler frequency offsets using GNSS coordinates and SIB19 ephemeris.
Part 3: Uplink Network Topology

The Two-Segment Uplink Flow

Cooperative Handset-to-Space Upload Path

Just as the downlink beams signals down, the **Uplink path (UL)** handles the reverse communication flow from your handheld phone back into the core 5G network:

📡 Segment 1: Uplink Service Link (UE-to-Sat) The smartphone (UE) transmits its upload signal upward over S-band. To prevent collisions with other users, **the UE pre-distorts its transmit timing (Common TA) and pre-shifts its frequency (Doppler pre-compensation)**. When the signal hits the satellite array, it aligns perfectly at 2.0 GHz, preserving UL-OFDMA.
🚀 Segment 2: Uplink Feeder Link (Sat-to-Gateway) The satellite aggregates the synchronized uplink carrier signals and relays them down to the NTN Ground Gateway antenna over Ka-band. The base station (gNB) then decodes the baseband frames and routes them into the terrestrial Core network.

Part 6: Timing Control (Timing Advance)

Autonomous Timing Advance (TA)

Overcoming Multi-Millisecond Delays

A standard terrestrial 5G tower has a round-trip time below 1ms. An NTN LEO satellite has upwards of 40ms, making conventional base-station timing loops useless.

3GPP Rel-17 shifts the delay calculation completely to the UE handset, using a cooperative approach:

Tier 1: Common TA (Handset Calculated) Based on SIB19 satellite ephemeris + active GNSS positions, the UE calculates the physical path distance and shifts its TX packets forward by up to 40ms.
Tier 2: Differential TA (gNB Commanded) Minor residual drifts are monitored at the gNB and fine-tuned via traditional closed-loop Timing Advance MAC CE commands.

Part 11: Spot Beam & Capacity Management

Spot Beams & Beam Hopping

Phased Array Spectrum Reuse

A single satellite covers a massive geographic footprint. To maximize capacity and power efficiency, satellites project focused "Spot Beams" (similar to ground-based cells) using phased-array antennas:

📡 Spatial Frequency Reuse Patterns To prevent adjacent spot beams from interfering with each other, satellites employ **Multi-Cell Frequency Reuse** (e.g. FR-4). Surrounding spot beams are allocated different frequencies/subbands (color-coded below) so adjacent cells never collide.
⚡ Dynamic Beam Hopping (Rel-18) Instead of wasting solar power illuminating empty deserts or oceans, the satellite dynamically **hops its beams in microseconds**. It flashes power and spectrum on high-traffic clusters (airports, trans-oceanic lanes) and dims over inactive cells to optimize energy.
Part 12: LEO Mobility & Handovers

LEO Mobility & Seamless Handovers

The Ephemeris-Driven Handover Paradigm

Because LEO satellites orbit at extreme velocities (~7.5 km/s), they stay in the line-of-sight of a static ground receiver for only **5 to 10 minutes** before sinking below the horizon. This introduces a major mobility challenge:

⏱️ The Visibility Window Terrestrial handovers are reactive—triggered when signal strength (RSRP) drops. In LEO NTN, waiting for a signal drop-off before initiating a handover results in connection drops over huge geographical boundaries.
📅 Ephemeris-Driven Time Handovers Instead of signal degradation, 3GPP Rel-18 uses **time-based, proactive handovers**. Handsets use orbital ephemeris data (SIB19) and absolute UTC time to calculate exactly when Satellite A will set. At that precise split-second, the handset seamlessly swaps its radio beams to the rising Satellite B without losing a single packet.
